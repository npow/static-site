The shape of the industry

~2,600 companies across 24 use-case segments. The biggest pools aren't humanoids — they're drones, mining/subsea/hazardous, warehouse AMRs, and consumer home. Robotics is far more industrial and mundane than the humanoid headlines suggest.

Takeaway: the headlines are humanoids; the volume is logistics, inspection, and dull-dangerous work.

Where the money is

Disclosed capital by segment, colored by our independent-business verdict (YES / emerging / partial / no). Capital piles into humanoids, robotaxis/AVs, and surgical — the most vertically-integrated, hardest-to-disrupt corners.

Takeaway: the best-funded segments are the ones where the maker owns the whole stack — capital is chasing integration, not an independent software layer.

The funding race

The most-capitalized companies in the census — overwhelmingly full-stack model+robot builders (humanoids, AVs, defense), not serving/middleware plays.

Takeaway: the giants all own model and fleet together; nobody got big selling a thinking layer to others.

When it was built

The founding wave: most of today's robotics companies are young — a surge clustered in the last decade, accelerating into the 2020s as learning-based control and cheap compute lowered the bar.

Takeaway: this is a young, fast-forming industry — most of the map didn't exist 10 years ago.

Companies by founding year (where known, 1990–2026).

Where it's being built

A tri-pole: the US, China, and Europe dominate, with a dense second tier in Korea, Japan, and Israel. China's robotics base is large but largely closed to a Western software vendor.

Takeaway: three centers of gravity — and the biggest one (China) is mostly unaddressable for a Western cloud play.

Where the brain runs — the question that decides the business

For each segment, where the thinking layer (the 1–10 Hz reasoning/planning) runs today, and whether an independent AI business exists. Almost everything is onboard or hybrid; genuinely cloud-native independent businesses are rare.

Takeaway: the cloud-serviceable, independently-sellable slice is small — most autonomy runs on the robot, and where it doesn't, the makers build it.

So what?

The demand to run robot AI off the device is real and broad — but a defensible, independent business selling it is narrow. The capital, the talent, and the buyers all cluster around owning model and robot together. The opening for a "rent the brain" company is a wedge, not a wave.
