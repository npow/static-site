Should you build a "fal for robotics"?

Can you run robots' AI in the cloud and sell it as a service — the honest case. May 2026.

A top-to-bottom read: the answer first, then the reasoning, then every robot type checked one by one, then — if you'd build it anyway — where to start and how big it gets.

The short answer

fal rents cloud GPUs to run AI models for images and video. The idea here is the same one for robots: run a robot's AI "brain" in the cloud and charge for it. To test whether that's a business, we walked through every kind of robot and the real companies building each one.

What we found: the demand to run robot AI off the device is real and growing — but a defensible independent business that does it in the cloud is rare. Not because it's impossible: a robot can run its own real-time reflexes and still ship the heavier thinking layer to your cloud — that hybrid split is the “fal for robotics” product. The real question is whether anyone would prefer to rent that from an independent company. Mostly they wouldn't — where offload works, robot makers build the AI themselves, get it free, or guard it as their edge; and the cases where cloud help would matter most (real-time control, or weak/jammed networks) are exactly the ones physics pushes back onto the robot. Of 24 robot types, only four support an independent AI business at all, and only one is genuinely cloud-based. The winners own the robots and the AI together.

So, as a pure cloud-serving company: not today. But the picture has real seams — places a focused team can build something. The rest of this page shows the reasoning, the evidence company by company, and where you'd start if you did it.

First, the one idea everything rests on

Every robot runs AI in three different places. They have completely different needs, and only the middle one is even a candidate for the cloud.

The answer, in full

The cloud opportunity is real and broad as a place where work happens — across most robots, the thinking layer and the training both pull compute off the device. But an independent, defensible business selling that is narrow. When we checked all 24 robot types against the actual companies, only four have a proven independent AI business today: commercial cleaning, inspection, drones, and defense. And of those, only inspection analytics is genuinely cloud-based — cleaning is a hybrid (control onboard, fleet data in the cloud) and the high-value drone and defense autonomy runs on the robot itself. So for a literal "run robots' AI in the cloud and charge for it" company, the proven count is about one.

Everywhere else, the AI is built in-house by the robot maker, taken free from open-source, bundled in by a model lab, or wrapped into a full rent-a-robot service. The companies actually positioned to win are the ones building the AI model and the robot fleet together — Physical Intelligence, Skild, Figure, Sereact — not a serving middleman.

What would turn this from "wait" into "go"? A few specific things — and today none of them is true. Here's what we're watching: on the right, the reasons it's a hard no right now; on the left, the signals that would change the call.

Two forces sit behind the "no." First, the money pools at the edges of a serving business, not in it — the chipmaker (NVIDIA) gives the serving software away to sell silicon, open-source projects make it free, and the model labs bundle it with their weights; a middleman in between gets squeezed to commodity margins. Second, the one durable move — owning the AI model and the robots — costs upward of a billion dollars (PI ~$11B, Skild ~$14B, Figure ~$39B are funded on exactly that), which is a different game than a serving startup. So the bar isn't "build good serving software" — it's "own the model and the fleet," and that's a $1B+ bet.

Two questions decide it. First: is the AI your moat, or just plumbing? If it's a genuine competitive edge — something customers feel and rivals can't copy — the robot maker builds it in-house and there's no room for you. It's "plumbing" (worth sourcing) only if every test below fails.

Serving is CORE → build in-house (any one)Serving is CONTEXT → source it (all of these)
Measurable competitive weapon — your latency / throughput / $-per-inference beats rivals and customers feel it (higher pick rate, lower $/pick, more uptime)Customers neither see nor pay for it — undifferentiated plumbing
Embeds proprietary IP — custom kernels, distillation, action-head runtime, model-specific scheduler that competitors can't copyAn off-the-shelf runtime (vLLM / TensorRT / NIM / openpi) already hits your required inference rate
Safety / certification-critical and you must own the evidence chain + carry the liabilityNo proprietary IP is lost by outsourcing it
Co-designed with your model — runtime and weights can't be cleanly separatedCleanly separable from your model (stable API boundary)
Owning it changes your unit economics at your scale (already at graduation scale — see the reference section at the end)Below graduation scale — owning it changes nothing yet

Second, even if it is plumbing: cheaper to build or to buy? Free open-source has made building cheap, so the buy case only wins below real scale, when speed matters, or when requirements are still moving. At scale, buyers graduate to building it themselves.

One objection hangs over both rankings: those revenue ramps and windows look slow for an AI company in 2026 — wouldn't AI compress them? On the building side, yes — a five-to-ten-person team can now build an autonomy stack that once took fifty engineers, which is partly why a small entrant is even viable. On the revenue side, no: robots ship at factory speed, safety and aviation certifications run on a regulator's calendar, and defense buys at government pace. The AI-app records (a coding tool hitting $100M in a year) don't transfer to physical, regulated systems. So the first year or two of each ramp holds as shown; the upside is in the later tail. Investors do count on the speed — but they price it into valuations (Anduril ~$30B+, Shield AI ~$12.7B), not into revenue curves.
