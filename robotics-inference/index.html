Should you build a "fal for robotics"?

Can you run robots' AI in the cloud and sell it as a service — the honest case. May 2026.

A top-to-bottom read: the answer first, then the reasoning, then every robot type checked one by one, then — if you'd build it anyway — where to start and how big it gets.

The short answer#

fal rents cloud GPUs to run AI models for images and video. The idea here is the same one for robots: run a robot's AI "brain" in the cloud and charge for it. To test whether that's a business, we walked through every kind of robot and the real companies building each one.

What we found: the demand to run robot AI off the device is real and growing — but a defensible independent business that does it in the cloud is rare. Not because it's impossible: a robot can run its own real-time reflexes and still ship the heavier thinking layer to your cloud — that hybrid split is the “fal for robotics” product. The real question is whether anyone would prefer to rent that from an independent company, and mostly they wouldn't. Of the 30 best-fit buyers we screen later, only ~3 currently buy a brain externally (inferred posture, not validated procurement); where offload works, robot makers build the AI themselves, get it free, or guard it as their edge. It helps to separate two value axes. Value-per-real-time-decision (real-time control, weak/jammed links) does get pushed back onto the robot. But value-of-aggregation (fleet-learning, long-horizon planning, semantic understanding) is both high-value and latency-tolerant, so that slice is genuinely cloud-eligible — the narrow part is that the makers mostly capture it themselves. Of 24 robot types, only four support an independent AI business at all, and only one is genuinely cloud-based. The winners own the robots and the AI together.

So, as a pure cloud-serving company: not today. But the picture has real seams — places a focused team can build something. The catch: it’s a build-heavy market (most makers build their own brain), so the motion isn’t selling to people shopping for a brain — it’s intercepting or de-risking a build, a harder sale aimed at the adjacent layer they haven’t staffed. This intercept-a-build motion is the central unproven risk of the whole thesis: we found no cited precedent of a funded robotics team abandoning an in-progress build to rent, switching costs cut against it, and "we own our AI" is a story investors reward — so treat it as the bet to validate, not a settled go-to-market. The rest of this page shows the reasoning, the evidence company by company, and where you'd start if you did it.

First, the one idea everything rests on#

A robot's AI usefully decomposes into three places — when the architecture keeps them separate. They have different needs, and the middle one is the main candidate for the cloud. The catch: the boundary between fast control and the thinking layer depends on architecture, network and safety, and it keeps moving — end-to-end VLAs can blur the two (see A3.5: action chunks execute at 50–200 Hz). So a clean middle to lift to the cloud isn't guaranteed for every robot.

The answer, in full#

The cloud opportunity is real and broad as a place where work happens — across most robots, the thinking layer and the training both pull compute off the device. But an independent, defensible business selling that is narrow. When we checked all 24 robot types against the actual companies, only four have a proven independent AI business today: commercial cleaning, inspection, drones, and defense. The product can ship in several deployment modes — far-cloud, on-prem/near-edge, hybrid (control onboard, thinking/fleet data off-device), or analytics-only. Under the strict pure-cloud definition, only inspection analytics qualifies — cleaning is a hybrid (control onboard, fleet data in the cloud), and the high-value drone and defense autonomy runs on the robot itself — so the strict count is about one. But that hybrid split is the product this page actually defines, and counted that way the set is larger — cleaning, inspection, and parts of drones — so the strict definition narrows the thesis without killing it.

Everywhere else, the AI is built in-house by the robot maker, taken free from open-source, bundled in by a model lab, or wrapped into a full rent-a-robot service. The companies positioned to win today are the ones building the AI model and the robot fleet together — Physical Intelligence, Skild, Figure, Sereact — not a serving middleman. That's not a settled endpoint, though: stacks often disaggregate over time (IBM→Wintel), and if interface standards fragment off NVIDIA/HF (GO gate G3), an independent layer could open.

What would turn this from "wait" into "go"? A few specific things — and today none of them is true. Here's what we're watching: on the right, the reasons it's a hard no right now; on the left, the signals that would change the call.

Two forces sit behind the "no," but be precise about which, because the easy version is wrong. The wrong version is "serving is free, so there's no margin": NVIDIA gives the serving software away to sell silicon, open-source projects make it free, the model labs bundle it. But free software still has to run on GPUs that someone provisions, autoscales, keeps up and optimizes — and that hosting-plus-optimization layer is exactly what fal, Modal and Together charge billions for. Robot inference needs real specialized work on top — real-time action-chunking, streaming multimodal sensors, deterministic latency, a clean edge/cloud split — that a generic LLM-serving project won't hand you. So the supply side is buildable but not automatically defensible — a moat would have to come from data rights, switching costs, or proprietary runtime IP, and the KILL gates below say those aren't in hand yet. The real "no" is on the demand side: the highest-value inference runs onboard (real-time loops, contested links), so the cloud-serviceable slice is narrow to begin with, and within it the robot makers overwhelmingly build or bundle the brain themselves (of the 30 best-fit buyers we screened, 20 build in-house). NVIDIA, open source and the labs don't squeeze your margin so much as hand every would-be customer a good-enough free default, so almost no one is shopping to rent. The second force is real and unchanged: the one move that captures the whole stack — owning the AI model and the robots — is what PI (~$11B), Skild (~$14B) and Figure (~$39B) are valued at, which is what investors price the move at, not what it costs to make. The capital to enter a narrow wedge is far smaller — a 5–10-person team can build an autonomy stack — so the gate is strategic (owning model and fleet), not a literal $1B build. The bar, then, isn't "build good serving software" (you can); it's that the rentable demand is thin (onboard placement + makers build), and the move that captures the full stack is a $1B+ bet.

One nuance the tree builds in: a robot program's stage only sets a starting lean. Early on — a handful of units, a human still in the loop — work leans off-robot. Two questions decide it. First: is the AI your moat, or just plumbing? "Core" doesn't automatically mean "build" — it sets the default toward owning it, but the real call is graded (own / co-develop / license / managed-service), weighing strategic importance against internal capability, any vendor speed/cost/certification advantage, and switching- and re-cert risk. If it's a genuine competitive edge — something customers feel and rivals can't copy — the lean is to build or co-develop in-house, and the room for an independent shrinks (firms still outsource even strategic pieces when a vendor is decisively better, faster, or already certified — which is exactly the intercept-a-build thesis below). It tilts toward sourcing only if every test below fails.

Second, even if it is plumbing: cheaper to build or to buy? Free open-source lowers the acquisition cost (no license fee) — but not the real TCO: infra, autoscaling, on-call ops, validation, observability, safety and support all remain. The buy case wins below real scale, when speed matters, or when requirements are still moving. The intuition that buyers "graduate to building it themselves" at scale is an assumption, not a law — it needs cohort evidence, and some scaled buyers instead standardize on a vendor precisely to stay focused on their core.

One objection hangs over both rankings: those revenue ramps and windows look slow for an AI company in 2026 — wouldn't AI compress them? Who would actually buy it? — the ICP shortlist#

Method. We screened all 2,584 companies in the directory (3,074 placements across segments/regions) — each tagged with what it does, funding, stage and region — segment by segment, against one question: would this company rent a cloud thinking layer, or build its own? This is a best-effort full-census screen across the directory as constructed, not a hand-picked sample. But the directory itself was built from public sources (Crunchbase, company sites, industry databases) with standard deduplication, so coverage is broad but not provably exhaustive — smaller or stealth-mode companies are likely undercounted. The ideal customer is a Seed-to-Series-C robot OEM or operator with a credible body but no frontier research org, selling into a latency-tolerant, non-regulated domain where the 1-10 Hz reasoning layer is the differentiator they must ship fast — and would rather rent than build. (In reality — see the acquisition playbook below — 20 of the 30 are building in-house today; this screen flags who has the strongest economic reason to rent, and the playbook is how we’d actually convert that.)

The ICP is a "rent-the-brain" robot company, not a brain-builder — but in practice most build anyway (see the acquisition playbook below), so the real target is a builder you intercept, not a shopper. Concretely: a hardware-first OEM or a robot-as-a-service operator whose value proposition is the physical machine, the deployment, or the service contract — but whose competitive gap is autonomy. They have enough capital to pay for a usage-priced cloud reasoning layer, but not the budget to staff a frontier VLA/foundation-model team — yet the screen below shows most build some autonomy in-house anyway. So the sweet spot is not "can pay, won't build"; it is "can pay, and currently DOES build — the intercept target": a funded team already staffing an autonomy effort where the bet is that renting the 1-10 Hz reasoning layer is cheaper or faster than continuing that build. That renting-beats-building claim is the thesis, and it is unproven (see H12 / the playbook).

Funding band (a heuristic, not a law): roughly $5M-$150M, concentrated in Seed through Series C, is where the economic case is densest — but it is a tendency, not a rule. Below ~$2M (e.g. Eliport at $0.16M, Ubica at $0.76M) the fit is structurally right but the company usually cannot pay — though some tiny firms still buy a narrow long-tail layer. Above ~$200-300M (Dexterity $300M, Symbotic, Figure, Skild) the company has the cash to build its own stack and usually does, though a well-funded firm can still choose to buy an adjacent layer. The densest pockets sit at Seed-Series B in the $10-90M range. Caveat: ability-to-pay is unvalidated for the ~40-45% of named targets with no disclosed funding — for them the "can pay" half of the thesis is inferred from stage/segment, not confirmed.

Stage trigger: the buyer is in scaling mode under speed-to-autonomy pressure — racing to expand SKU/task coverage, site count, or fleet density faster than an in-house research hire could deliver. The decisive signal is architecture: they already run hybrid or teleop-assisted control today — transitional postures inside otherwise build-heavy players (Agtonomy's tele-assist, Reflex Robotics'/temi's teleop-to-autonomy, Coco's operator-assist, ICE Cobotics' subscription RaaS) — so cloud/hybrid cognition fits their existing posture rather than requiring a re-architecture.

Domain trigger: the workload must be latency-tolerant and non-safety-critical at the reasoning tier — coverage/route planning, grasp-and-sequence reasoning, social/conversational dialog, planogram diffing, mission orchestration — running at 1-10 Hz while hard-real-time control stays onboard. The single cleanest proof point is commercial cleaning, where Brain Corp's BrainOS already licenses a thinking layer to Tennant, Nilfisk, SoftBank, ICE and RPS — demonstrating the buy-not-build behavior is real and repeatable.

Our unique value proposition.

A managed, hosted cloud reasoning brain for robots — the "thinking layer" you rent instead of staff. It runs the 1-10 Hz task-level planning, decision, dialogue and fleet-orchestration that sits above a robot's edge perception/control, hybrid edge/cloud and embodiment-agnostic, so any robotics company ships smarter autonomy and new coverage in quarters — without building, funding, or maintaining a frontier-model research org. Keep your edge nav and your moat; rent the brain you can't afford to staff, and redirect scarce ML headcount to hardware, data and GTM. fal-for-robotics: cognition as a service, billed per robot or per decision, not per research hire.

Play 1 — Abandon-build (vs in-house builders, ~20 targets). Core argument: a seed/Series-A/B hardware team cannot out-spend Physical Intelligence / NVIDIA on a foundation-model research org, so renting the 1-10 Hz reasoning brain ships autonomy quarters faster and redeploys scarce ML headcount onto the true moat (hardware, actuation, data, GTM). Caveats — read before acting on this.

Funding data is materially incomplete. A large share of the most "buy-not-build"-shaped companies — bootstrapped/private OEMs (most of commercial cleaning: Tennant, Kärcher, Nilfisk, RPS, Hako, Comac, Fimap), undisclosed-funding makers, and many seed players — have null funding figures, so the "can pay" half of the thesis is unverified for them. Roughly 40-45% of named targets lack a disclosed funding number; ability-to-pay is inferred from stage/segment, not confirmed.

The two best-fit segments are the smallest pools. The strong-rated beachheads (commercial cleaning, social/service) are genuinely fertile but narrow: in cleaning, the biggest names are public/private incumbents — build-the-machine, rent-the-intelligence is proven but the buyer count is finite — and social/care skews to thinly-funded seed companies where ability-to-pay is the binding constraint. Several of the largest segments by company count (drones, AMRs, defense, subsea) are mostly NON-ICP once builders, competitors, suppliers and regulation-locked players are removed.

Many high-scoring targets carry partial-overlap or competitor risk. Path Robotics ($271M), Nimble ($221M), Telexistence, Simbe and NEURA are at or past the build-vs-buy line and may build in-house; Augmentus and Wandelbots are themselves programming-software vendors (partial competitors). Scores in the 60s-70s are explicitly hedged in the source data.

Geography injects a cloud-data-residency tax. China targets (Elite, AUBO, JAKA, FAIRINO, Rokae, Robot Era, Pudu, Multiway, Guozi) score lower because of data-locality pressure and domestic-vendor/foundation-model preference; a Western cloud brain may be a poor fit regardless of the technical match.

"Cloud-fit" is hybrid, not pure-cloud, almost everywhere. Fast control stays onboard in every segment; only the 1-10 Hz reasoning tier is rentable, and even that often wants an on-prem/edge option (hospital HIPAA, security surveillance feeds, China residency). The product must support hybrid deployment, and every deal in security/patrol and hospital must clear an on-prem objection.

The screen is a desk-research snapshot, not validated demand. Fit scores reflect inferred build-vs-buy economics and architecture, not confirmed buying intent, budget authority, or a stated willingness to depend on a third-party cloud for autonomy — all of which require direct discovery before treating any named target as qualified.
